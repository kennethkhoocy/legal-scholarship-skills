# Law-review structure — Shareholder Democracy (law & economics)

Single-author theory article, ~10,850 body words across six numbered Sections, from
Introduction through Conclusion. Argument arc: incumbent paradigm → gap → original framework →
descriptive application → normative reform → conclusion.

the most footnote-dense. A separate ~1,750-word alphabetical reference list follows Section 6.

## Heading and numbering conventions
Three-level multi-decimal arabic hierarchy: `Section N` → `N.M` subsection → `N.M.K`
subsubsection (e.g., 2.1, 3.2.4). Top-level unit is called **"Section," not "Part."** Headings
are short descriptive noun phrases naming the analytical object ("Voting and Participation
Rules," "Free-Riding Problems"); the two application Sections carry an argumentative frame in
a colon-appended tag ("...A Descriptive Account" / "...A Normative Account"). Not thesis-in-
heading. Only Section 3 (2 subsections, one with 4 subsubsections) and Section 2 go three levels
deep; Sections 4-5 stop at subsection.

## Introduction architecture
~8 paragraphs, in order:
1. **Hook + incumbent paradigm** (¶1-2): Blackstone "little republic" analogy; establishes that agency-cost theory dominates (Jensen-Meckling's 100,000+ cites), then expounds its premise.
2. **Existing critique** (¶3): scholars who already push back (Bainbridge; Blair & Stout).
3. **Gap** (¶4-5): the neglected distinction — agency-cost theory is *bilateral*; multilateral preference aggregation among heterogeneous shareholders is ignored. Sharpened by the shareholders-as-voters vs. shareholders-as-employer analogy.
4. **Thesis + contribution** (¶6, ~⅔ through): "In this article, I... label 'the costs of collective decision-making'"; builds on Hansmann; explicit novelty claim ("to the best of my knowledge, a novel undertaking").
5. **Stakes / timeliness** (¶7): rise of institutional ownership (7%→70%; Big Three = largest holder in 88% of S&P 500).
6. **Roadmap** (¶8, final ¶): "My article is organized as follows. Section 2 examines... Section 3... Section 6 concludes." — one clause per Section.

Thesis lands mid-intro (¶6), not first; roadmap is the explicit final paragraph.

## Footnote architecture
Density **18.1 footnotes per 1,000 body words** (196 notes over Sections 1-6). Overall
**body:footnote word ratio ≈ 1 : 0.20** (footnotes are ~16% of body+note text) — light for the
genre; the article is body-driven with citation-only notes. Sampled classification of all 196:
- **Pure citation ~80%** (156): author-date short form ("Jensen and Meckling (1976)."), "Ibid." for repeats, cross-refs ("See Section 4."), and statutory/case cites (§ 144 Del. Code Ann.; *Kahn v. M & F Worldwide*).
- **Substantive-textual ~12%** (23; ~half also carry a cite): the argument/qualification lives in the note.
- **Mixed (brief prose gloss + cite) ~9%** (17).

Substantive notes hold: (a) **terminological/scope conventions** the author adopts ("I use 'manager' interchangeably with 'director'"; how "corporate law" is defined here); (b) **technical economic qualifications** (non-contractible = unobservable *or* unverifiable; failure of the Fisher separation theorem); (c) **extended theoretical elaboration** of a concept only gestured at in body (Tirole's active vs. speculative monitoring; a full worked principal-agent hypothetical); (d) **doctrinal/statutory/case detail** supporting a claim (majority-of-minority mechanics with US + UK authorities); (e) **counterarguments / distinct scholarly critiques** parked out of the main line (Raz's purpose-based critique; the point that prevailing scholarship ignores controlling-ownership efficiency gains). Main-line argument stays in body text; notes carry sourcing, asides, and qualifications.

## Per-Part internal organization
**Section 1 (Intro):** see above — paradigm → critique → gap → thesis → stakes → roadmap.
**Section 2 (Background):** layered build of the incumbent theory — 2.1 canonical Jensen-Meckling model → 2.2 extension (second-best, incomplete contracting) → 2.3 the pivot (why agency-cost logic breaks once parties are added). Formalized with region-labeled figures (Fig 1-2); heaviest foundational citation.
**Section 3 (Core theory):** 3.1 builds conceptual apparatus (issue space, preference space) with spatial-model figures + a Condorcet-cycle table; 3.2 the taxonomy — one subsubsection per friction (free-riding / rent-seeking / holdout / indeterminacy), each on a parallel template: define friction → mechanism distorting preference aggregation → corporate example or figure → qualification.
**Section 4 (Descriptive):** three parallel subsections, each "agency-cost theory struggles to explain X; the collective-decision framework explains it better," worked through US + UK (and France/Germany/Italy) doctrine; heavy statute/case footnotes.
**Section 5 (Normative):** two reform proposals, each: name the friction → current law → proposed reform → supporting/contrary evidence; comparative US vs UK.
**Section 6 (Conclusion):** recap thesis → flag scope limitation (assumed stable shareholder base) → open one future direction (exit rights, appraisal, Tiebout sorting).

## Signposting
Roadmap is an explicit one-clause-per-Section paragraph closing the intro. Each Section opens by
tying back to the prior ("In Section 3, I introduced...") and mini-previews its own subunits
("I explore four critical frictions..."; "I provide three examples"; "I offer two proposals").
Dense bidirectional cross-references throughout ("See Section 4.", "As I will explain later",
"In the next section"), many carried in footnotes.

## Idiosyncrasies (do not generalize)
- **Citation system is economics/European-L&E, not US Bluebook.** Scholarship cited author-date in footnotes with a terminal alphabetical reference list; "Ibid." (not *Id.*/*supra*) for repeats; only primary law (statutes/cases) uses conventional legal citation. Do **not** default an article plan to full-Bluebook notes or a US-law-review-scale footnote apparatus.
- Top-level unit is "Section," and there is **no "Part" superstructure** — a flat 6-Section spine.
- Heavy formal apparatus unusual for a law review: five figures (region/spatial diagrams), a preference-cycle Table, math notation, "Region A-D" labels.
- Comparative US/UK/continental framing throughout (not single-jurisdiction doctrinal).
- One figure (Fig 4) is embedded *inside* a footnote; pandoc conversion left empty `\section{}` markers and put the author affiliation in footnote 1 — source artifacts, not design choices.
